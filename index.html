    How Are Prayer Times Calculated?

    Islamic prayer times come from astronomy — from where the sun sits relative to the horizon at a given place. Fajr and Isha depend on how far the sun is below the horizon during twilight, Dhuhr begins just after the sun passes its highest point, and Maghrib starts at sunset. Because these depend on latitude, longitude and the date, prayer times shift a little every day and differ from one city to another.

    Different recognized authorities use slightly different twilight angles, which is why times can vary by a few minutes between methods. Widely used ones include the Muslim World League (MWL), the Islamic Society of North America (ISNA), the Egyptian General Authority of Survey, and Umm al-Qura University in Makkah. Picking the method your local community follows gives the most accurate result.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    How are prayer times determined?

    They are calculated from the sun's position for a given location and date — primarily the angles of twilight for Fajr and Isha, solar noon for Dhuhr, and sunset for Maghrib.

    Why do prayer times differ between apps or mosques?

    Different authorities use different twilight angles and conventions. A few minutes of variation between methods such as MWL, ISNA, or Umm al-Qura is normal and expected.

    Which calculation method should I choose?

    The best choice is the method officially followed in your country or by your local mosque, as it reflects the convention your community relies on.
